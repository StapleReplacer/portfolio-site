There are many myths which perpetuate misleading information and stigma about Alzheimer's disease and other forms of dementia.

Question 1:
Identify which of the questions below are true or false:

Dementia is a natural part of ageing.

False, Dementia is not a natural part of ageing. Not everyone who grows old will develop dementia. Not everyone who develops dementia is old - people in their 20s have been diagnosed with dementia, although this is very rare.

Dementia is caused by diseases of the brain.

True, Dementia is the umbrella term for a number of neurological conditions, of which the major symptom is the decline in brain function due to physical changes in the brain.

One common type of dementia is Alzheimer's disease.

True, Alzeimer’s disease is the most common type of dementia, however Alzheimer’s is just one type or cause of dementia.

Dementia is untreatable and cannot be slowed down.

False: There is no cure for dementia, but early symptoms can be managed through a combination of medication and lifestyle changes.

It is not possible to live well with dementia and remain in the community.

False, people with dementia may still be able to work, drive and have relationships. What they can do and how long they can do it for will depend on their particular circumstances.

Living well will mean different things for different people. We’re not saying that dementia doesn’t bring challenges, but despite these challenges it is still possible to live well with dementia.

Dementia is just about losing your memory.

False, there are a range of other brain functions affected by dementia. For example, communication, motor skills, sequencing, changes in inhibition and vision.

People with dementia can still communicate effectively.

True, some people with dementia can struggle with communication but there are lots of things we can do to help and adjust our communication style to assist them to understand.

All people with dementia experience loss of social relationships after being diagnosed with dementia.

False, while some people have lost contact with friends and family, others report that they have strengthened the bond with family since being diagnosed.
