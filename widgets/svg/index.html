Perception

The processing of sensory input may be affected. People living with dementia may feel cold when it is warm or depth perception may be affected (such as interpreting a dark coloured mat on the floor as a hole in the ground).

"It takes me longer to separate out visual distractions such as reflections from mirrors etc. from what is really happening. This makes visually busy environments like shopping centres very confusing."

Physical function

Over time physical changes may also include loss of mobility, continence, or fine motor skills. Sleep patterns are also commonly disrupted.

"Knives and forks just don’t seem right to hold and I often drop them"

Planning

Everything has to be planned – what to wear, what to eat, how long each task will take, the best route to travel, often what others need for their day. For the person living with dementia this can become difficult and may also experience difficulty making decisions.

"My ability to go with the flow is gone."

Insight

This is the ability to see ourselves as others do. To understand if we are performing all our daily tasks effectively and appropriately. The person living with dementia may not recognise that there is anything wrong with a situation, such as not completing tasks safely.

"My sense of values are changing. Somethings I would have done without hesitation are now overwhelming, likewise some tasks that I once thought of as being very risky don’t concern me now."

Language

The person living with dementia may begin to talk less or have difficulty finding the right word; they may become increasingly vague in communication, or have difficulty understanding what is said or being asked.

"I have great difficulty in following a conversation, particularly in a noisy environment. Sometimes I can’t find the right word when I need it, but it might come to me an hour or two later."

Emotions

Emotional responses to people and events may be magnified. Part of the frontal lobe (responsible for this control) may be damaged.

"Good music makes me cry. Not out of sorrow, but out of joy!"

Behaviour

In some forms of dementia behaviour change is the first symptom noticed. The person living with dementia may act differently or changes in personality may become more obvious as the dementia progresses.

"I used to read a couple of books a week, now I have been slowly reading the same book for almost twelve months. I will finish it!"

Thinking

The person’s ability to process and analyse information or concepts may be impaired. They may no longer be able to add, subtract or balance a cheque book (when previously worked as an accountant), or to problem solve when they are having difficulty. Confusion about the day, time, where they live, where they have come from, what they have just been doing, or the actual situation they find themselves in.

"I often don’t know what day it is. But this rarely matters. I think other people are just jealous, because to me every day is like the weekend – no work days."

Memory

Recent term memory is affected in Alzheimer’s and some other types of dementia. The person living with dementia may forget a recent conversation or have no recollection of going to the shops earlier in the day.

"I can remember the registration number of the first car I owned in 1971, but I often forget my PIN number."

Attention

In earlier stages of dementia there may be difficulty concentrating on more than one task at a time.

"I sometimes have difficulty sticking to a task and will begin one thing and then move to another before the first is complete. When I do finish the task I feel I really have accomplished something worthwhile."
